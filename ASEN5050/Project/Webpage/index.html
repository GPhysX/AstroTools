Solar Sailing to the Outer Planets

John Clouse
ASEN 5050

Abstract

Most interplanetary spacecraft have used finite burns to escape Earth orbit and be captured by their destination's gravity. The propellant expended for these maneuvers is a large part of the mass and cost for these missions. If the hyperbolic excess velocity at departure and/or arrival could be reduced, propellant mass could be saved. Solar sails are an attractive technology to potentially lessen propellant expenditure. Solar sails convert solar photon momentum to spacecraft acceleration, providing a free – and potentially continuous – propulsion source. This propulsion system is only limited by sail size, reflective parameters, and orientation to the sun. This project will seek to determine the savings in hyperbolic excess velocity at departure and arrival for a baseline spacecraft and steering angle.

Solar Sails

Solar sails use solar photon momentum to accelerate the spacecraft. The solar flux has an inverse-square relationship to the distance from the sun, just like gravitation. Therefore, the maximum acceleration provided by the sail and the acceleration due to solar gravity can form the ratio β[ROSS].

Beta = EQN 1

\[\beta=\frac{a_{srp}}{a_{grav}}\]

The relationship in Eqn 1 provides the mission designer the ability to design trajectories without prior knowledge of spacecraft mass, sail area, and sail reflective parameters. It also allows the acceleration due to solar radiation pressure be expressed in terms of gravitational acceleration [McInnes].

The angle between the sun vector and the sail-normal vector is called the coning angle, α. This parameter is steered to produce the sailforce in the desired direction. However, it is not optimal to just steer to the coning angle between 0 and π/2 . Take the sail force direction:

EQN 2

\[\bf n=\cos\alpha\hat{r}+\sin\alpha\hat{p}\times\hat{p}\]

and the vector that maximum sail force is to be applied:

EQN 3

\[\bf q=\cos\tilde{\alpha}\hat{r}+\sin\tilde{\alpha}\hat{p}\times\hat{p}\]

The force magnitude fq is [McInnes]

EQN 4

\[ f_q=2PA( \textbf n\cdot \hat{\textbf r} )^2(\textbf n\cdot \textbf q)\]

By combining Eqns 2-4 and setting the result's derivative equal to zero, the optimal coning angle is found in the following expression:

EQN 5

\[\tan\alpha_{opt}=\frac{-3+\sqrt{9+8\tan^2\tilde{\alpha}}}{4\tan{\tilde{\alpha}}}\]

The cone angle is shown against the desired force angle in FIGURE. Due to the transverse force component being limited by the product of sinα and cosα , one can see that maximizing the force in the transverse direction (with respect to the Sun) requires a coning angle of 35.25○

The 2D polar equations of motion for a large gravitating body and solar radiation pressure are given by [McInnes]:

EQN 6

\[\ddot{r}-r\dot{\theta}^2=\frac{\mu}{r^2}(\beta\cos^3\alpha-1)\\ r\theta+2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}=\beta\frac{\mu}{r^2}\cos^2\alpha\sin\alpha\]

From Eqns 2 and 3, it is evident that α provides no sail force at π/2.

Optimal Trajectories

Optimal trajectories can be found with a variation of parameters (VOP) technique. To optimally increase an orbital parameter, one may take the VOP for that equation and solve for α [McInnes] by means of

EQN 8

\[f_\lambda=2PA( \textbf n\cdot \hat{\textbf r} )^2(\textbf n\cdot \lambda)\]

For the orbit semi-major axis to increase, the expression for α is found to be

\[\tan\tilde{\alpha}=\frac{1+e\cos f}{e\sin f}\]

Simulation

Solar sail trajectories for various values of β were generated to optimally increase the spacecraft semi-major axis to that of Mars and Jupiter. Planetary orbits are assumed to be coplanar and circular. Perturbing accelerations were not considered, just those of the Sun's gravity and solar radiation pressure. Trajectories are assumed to start outside of Earth's sphere of influence, where Vinfinity is zero. Thus, the only delta-V needed in these trajectories is to inject the spacecraft in the target planet's orbit. Resultant trajectories can be seen for Mars in Figures X-Y.

Table X shows the performance for these trajectories compared to a Hohmann transfer.

Trajectories where the semi-major axis is optimally increased can provide advantages in delta-V savings over pure Hohmann transfers. For the most inefficient sail configuration simulated, more than 50% of delta-V is saved at the cost of almost tripling the transfer time. For β=0.10, the transfer time is similar, but the continuous sail thrust saves over 1.1 km/s. This is not as attractive as it seems, however, due to the greater sail area required and the challenges in implementing it. β=0.15 actually saves two months of transfer time, but the delta-V exceeds the Hohmann transfer's.

Trajectories to Jupiter where semi-major-axis optimally increased show fewer desirable qualities than those of Mars. In the case of β=0.05, the transfer time is over ten times longer and the spacecraft would have to carry more fuel than one completing a Hohmann transfer, or use a creative scheme to reduce the chemically-induced delta-V. There is no case where solely optimizing semi-major axis reaches Jupiter in less time than a Hohmann transfer, and the required delta-V for planetary capture increases with sail efficiency.
